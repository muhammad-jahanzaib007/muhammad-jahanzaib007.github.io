Why Your Cross-Validation Is Probably Too Optimistic

Most cross-validation numbers quietly leak information from the future or from correlated samples. Here is how that happens and what to do about it.

The number on the slide is rarely the number in production

I have lost count of the times a model scored beautifully under cross-validation and then quietly disappointed everyone once it met real data. The usual explanation offered is distribution shift, and sometimes that is fair. But in my experience the more common culprit is simpler and more embarrassing: the cross-validation procedure itself was leaking information it should not have had. The score was never an honest estimate of generalisation in the first place.

This matters because cross-validation is the tool we reach for precisely when we want to trust a number before deployment. If that number is inflated, every decision built on top of it inherits the inflation: which model to ship, which features to keep, which hyperparameters to trust, how much budget to justify. An optimistic estimate is not a harmless rounding error, it is a systematic bias that tends to grow exactly when the stakes are highest.

The good news is that the failure modes are well understood and mostly mechanical. Once you know what to look for, spotting an inflated cross-validation setup becomes almost pattern matching. The bad news is that the standard k-fold recipe taught in most introductions does nothing to protect you from the two biggest offenders: temporal leakage and dependent samples.

Leakage through preprocessing you did before splitting

The classic mistake is fitting a preprocessing step on the whole dataset before splitting into folds. Suppose you standardise your features by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, and you compute that mean and standard deviation using all the data. Then you run five-fold cross-validation. Each fold's test set has already influenced the scaling statistics, because it contributed to the mean and variance used to transform itself. The leakage is small in magnitude for simple scaling, but it is leakage nonetheless, and it compounds when the preprocessing is more aggressive.

Feature selection is where this really bites. Imagine a dataset with two thousand candidate features and only three hundred rows, a fairly common shape in genomics-style or wide tabular problems. If you compute correlation with the target across the full dataset and keep the top fifty features, then cross-validate a model using only those fifty, you have already shown every fold's test set to the feature selection step. The selection process was allowed to peek at the labels it will later be scored against. I have seen this produce cross-validated accuracy above ninety percent on a problem where the true signal, evaluated properly with selection inside each fold, supported something closer to sixty.

The fix is unglamorous but non-negotiable: any step that learns from data, whether it is scaling, imputation, feature selection, dimensionality reduction, or target encoding, must be fitted only on the training portion of each fold and then applied unchanged to that fold's held-out portion. This is what pipeline objects in most machine learning libraries are designed to enforce, and the discipline of using them properly is worth far more than any amount of hyperparameter tuning.

Target encoding deserves a special mention because it is a leakage machine if used carelessly. Replacing a categorical value with the mean target for that category, computed on the full training set, injects target information into a feature. Inside a cross-validation fold this is fine only if the encoding is recomputed using solely that fold's training rows. Do it globally first and the encoded feature effectively contains a smoothed copy of the answer.

Dependent samples make folds less independent than they look

Random k-fold splitting assumes that rows are exchangeable, meaning any row could swap places with any other without changing the structure of the problem. Many real datasets violate this quietly. Consider a medical dataset where each patient contributes several visits as separate rows. If patient A's visits are scattered across both the training and test folds, the model can learn patient-specific patterns, things like baseline health markers or writing style in clinical notes, and then exploit that same patient's presence in the test fold. The score you get reflects the model's ability to recognise patients it has already met, not its ability to generalise to new ones.

I find it useful to run a small thought experiment with numbers. Suppose you have four hundred patients and two thousand visit-rows, an average of five visits each. A naive five-fold split at the row level puts roughly eighty percent of each patient's visits in the training folds and the rest in the test fold, virtually guaranteeing patient overlap. If patient identity correlates strongly with the outcome, even a weak model can achieve high apparent accuracy simply by memorising per-patient tendencies. Switching to a grouped split, where all of a patient's rows go entirely into either train or test, often drops the reported score by ten or more percentage points. That drop is not the model getting worse, it is the evaluation becoming honest.

The same problem appears with repeated measurements, sensor readings from the same device, images from the same subject captured at different angles, or text samples from the same author. Wherever there is a natural grouping variable that could let the model shortcut generalisation by recognising the group rather than solving the task, that variable needs to define the split boundaries, not be ignored by them.

Group-aware cross-validation is the standard remedy, and most libraries provide it directly. The mental check I always apply before trusting a score is simple: could two rows in different folds plausibly share a hidden identity, whether that is a person, a device, a session, or a physical location? If the answer is yes and the split does not respect that identity, the score deserves scepticism until proven otherwise.

Time is the leakage vector everyone forgets

Temporal data adds a subtler version of the dependency problem, because the direction of time is itself information. A standard random split on a dataset ordered by date will happily place a row from March in the training set and a row from January in the test set. The model then effectively gets to see the future before predicting the past, which is not a scenario it will ever encounter in production. Forecasting problems, churn prediction, fraud detection, and anything involving markets or user behaviour over time are all vulnerable to this.

Picture a churn model trained on eighteen months of customer data. A random five-fold split will mix months freely across folds. If the company changed its pricing structure in month ten, the model can learn that pattern from rows dated after month ten sitting in the training fold, then apply that knowledge to predict rows dated before month ten sitting in the test fold, which is backwards and impossible in reality. The reported accuracy might look stable and strong, perhaps eighty-five percent across folds, purely because the model is retroactively exploiting knowledge of events that had not yet happened relative to the rows it is scoring.

The correct approach is a forward-chaining or rolling-origin split, where each fold's training set consists only of data up to a certain point in time, and the test set is the period immediately following it. This mirrors how the model will actually be used: trained on history, evaluated on the near future. It is common to see performance drop noticeably once this discipline is applied, sometimes from mid-eighties percent accuracy down to somewhere in the seventies, and that drop is the true expected performance, not a defect introduced by the fix.

Small datasets amplify every one of these problems

With a few hundred or a few thousand rows, cross-validation scores already carry substantial variance from fold to fold, simply because each fold contains too few examples to average out noise. Layer leakage on top of that variance and you get a number that is both unstable and biased upward, a genuinely unhelpful combination. I have watched teams select a champion model because it scored two percentage points higher than a competitor, when the actual standard deviation across repeated cross-validation runs with different random seeds was closer to four percentage points. The selection was essentially noise dressed up as signal.

Repeated cross-validation, where the entire k-fold procedure is run several times with different random partitions and the results averaged, is a cheap and effective way to see this variance directly. If a model's score swings by several points depending on the random seed alone, that instability is itself important information, arguably more important than the headline average. It tells you the estimate is fragile and that small changes in the data or the split could flip your model ranking entirely.

A practical checklist before you trust a score

Before reporting or acting on a cross-validation result, I now run through a short set of questions, and I would encourage treating this as a habit rather than an occasional audit. First, does any preprocessing or feature selection step touch the full dataset before splitting, and if so, has it been moved inside a proper pipeline so it only sees training folds. Second, is there a grouping variable, whether a person, device, or session, that could let the model recognise identity rather than genuine pattern, and if so, has the split been made group-aware.

Third, does the data have a meaningful time ordering, and if it does, has the split respected that ordering with training strictly preceding testing in every fold. Fourth, has the procedure been repeated with multiple random seeds or multiple time windows to reveal how stable the score actually is, rather than trusting a single run. Fifth, and this one is easy to skip under deadline pressure, has a simple, well-understood baseline been evaluated under the exact same protocol, so that any apparent gain from a more complex model can be judged against a fair reference point rather than an inflated one.

None of this is exotic. It is mostly a matter of ordering operations correctly and being honest about the structure hidden in the data. But that ordering is precisely where optimism creeps in, quietly, one convenient shortcut at a time, until the number on the slide bears little resemblance to what happens after deployment.

The takeaway

Cross-validation is not a magic guarantee, it is a procedure that is only as trustworthy as the assumptions behind it, chiefly that folds are independent and that no information from the test portion has touched the training process. Every time those assumptions are violated, through global preprocessing, grouped samples, or ignored time order, the resulting score drifts upward and away from reality. The discipline of building leakage-proof pipelines, respecting grouping structure, honouring temporal order, and checking stability across repeated runs is not glamorous work, but it is the difference between a model that performs as advertised and one that quietly fails the moment it meets the real world. Treat every impressively high cross-validation score as a claim that needs checking, not a fact that has already been proven.
