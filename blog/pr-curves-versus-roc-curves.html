Precision-Recall Curves vs ROC Curves: When to Use Which

Both curves summarise a classifier across every threshold, but they tell very different stories when your classes are imbalanced. Here is how to pick the right one and read it correctly.

Why this choice keeps tripping people up

I have lost count of how many project reports I have seen where a model reports an ROC AUC of 0.95 and the author treats that as proof the model is excellent. Sometimes it is. Often, on closer inspection, the dataset is heavily imbalanced, the positive class is rare, and the ROC curve is quietly flattering a model that is actually not very useful in practice. This is not a niche gotcha; it is one of the most common ways evaluation goes wrong in applied machine learning, and it matters because the wrong curve can make a mediocre model look production-ready.

Both curves plot classifier performance across every possible decision threshold, which is exactly why they get treated as interchangeable. They are not. The receiver operating characteristic curve plots the true positive rate against the false positive rate. The precision-recall curve plots precision against recall, which is the same as true positive rate. The shared axis is recall or true positive rate; the difference lies entirely in what sits on the other axis, and that difference is where the story changes.

Getting this right is not about memorising a rule, it is about understanding what each curve is sensitive to. Once you see it with numbers, the choice becomes obvious rather than arbitrary.

The core mechanical difference

The false positive rate, used by ROC, is defined as false positives divided by the total number of actual negatives. Precision, used by PR curves, is defined as true positives divided by the total number of predicted positives, which is true positives plus false positives. The denominators are the crucial difference. ROC's false positive rate is normalised by the size of the negative class. Precision is normalised by however many positive predictions the model actually made.

When negatives vastly outnumber positives, that denominator difference becomes enormous. A modest absolute number of false positives can be a tiny fraction of a huge negative class, so the false positive rate barely moves and the ROC curve looks almost unaffected. That same number of false positives, however, can dwarf the small number of true positives, so precision collapses. The PR curve is reacting to the exact thing a ROC curve is structurally insensitive to.

This is not a flaw in ROC, it is simply what it was designed to measure, and in balanced settings, such as many medical imaging benchmarks with roughly equal classes, ROC behaves sensibly and precision would tell a similar story anyway. The trouble only starts when the base rate of the positive class becomes small.

A worked example with realistic numbers

Suppose you are building a fraud detection model. Out of 100,000 transactions, 500 are genuinely fraudulent, so the positive class rate is 0.5 percent, which is realistic for this domain. Say your model, at a chosen threshold, correctly flags 400 of those 500 frauds, so 400 true positives and 100 false negatives, giving a recall of 0.80.

Now suppose the model also flags 1,900 legitimate transactions as fraudulent, so 1,900 false positives, out of 99,500 genuine transactions. The false positive rate is 1,900 divided by 99,500, which is about 0.019, or 1.9 percent. On a ROC curve, a true positive rate of 0.80 paired with a false positive rate of 0.019 sits very close to the top-left corner, the ideal region. It looks fantastic.

Now look at precision. Total predicted positives are 400 true positives plus 1,900 false positives, which is 2,300. Precision is 400 divided by 2,300, which is roughly 0.17. In other words, when this model flags a transaction as fraud, it is right about 17 percent of the time, meaning over 8 in 10 flagged transactions are false alarms that a fraud analyst has to investigate and dismiss. That is an operationally painful model, and the ROC curve never told you so, because 1,900 false positives is a rounding error against 99,500 negatives, but it is nearly five times the number of true positives.

This is exactly the scenario where PR curves earn their keep. The gap between an ROC AUC of, say, 0.97 and a PR AUC of perhaps 0.35 for the same model is not a contradiction, it is two different questions being answered honestly.

When ROC is the right tool

ROC curves remain genuinely useful, and I would not want to leave the impression that they are obsolete or wrong. When classes are roughly balanced, or when you specifically care about how well a model separates classes independent of prevalence, ROC AUC is a clean, threshold-independent summary that is also relatively stable if the class balance shifts between training and deployment, since both axes are normalised within their own class.

ROC is also the natural choice when false positives and false negatives are both costed against their own respective class sizes rather than against each other directly, or when you are comparing models across datasets with different prevalence and want a metric that is less sensitive to that shift. It is well understood, widely reported, and comparable across a large body of prior work, which has genuine value for reproducibility and for situating a new result against published baselines.

Where ROC becomes misleading is specifically the rare-positive-class setting, and the mechanism is exactly the denominator issue described above. If you know your deployment setting has a rare positive class and the cost of chasing false alarms is real, treat a high ROC AUC with some scepticism and go look at precision directly.

Reading the PR curve properly

A precision-recall curve typically starts high on the left, at low recall, where the model is only committing to its most confident predictions, and tends to decline as recall increases and the threshold is relaxed to catch more positives at the cost of precision. The shape of that decline tells you about the operating trade-offs available: a curve that stays high for most of the recall range and only drops sharply near recall of 1.0 suggests a model with a genuinely strong separation for most cases, with a small residual of hard examples.

The baseline for a PR curve is not 0.5 the way it is for ROC. A random classifier's PR curve sits at a constant precision equal to the base rate of the positive class. In the fraud example above, a random classifier's precision would hover around 0.005, so a model achieving even 0.17 precision, however unimpressive it feels operationally, is still dramatically better than chance. That baseline shift is important context that is easy to miss if you are used to ROC's fixed 0.5 diagonal.

Average precision, the area under the PR curve computed as a weighted mean of precisions at each threshold, is the standard single-number summary, and it is worth reporting alongside a couple of concrete operating points, such as precision at a fixed recall of 0.8 or recall at a fixed precision of 0.9, because stakeholders generally reason more easily about a specific operating point than about an area under a curve.

A practical decision rule and the leakage-aware caveat

My rule of thumb is straightforward: check the prevalence of the positive class first. If it is roughly balanced, ROC AUC is a fine primary metric and PR curves add little extra insight. If the positive class is rare, which describes fraud detection, many medical screening tasks, anomaly detection, and most information retrieval settings, report the PR curve and average precision as the primary evidence, and keep ROC AUC as a secondary, supporting number rather than the headline claim.

It is also worth remembering that neither curve fixes a flawed evaluation split. If your train and test data leak information across a group, a time boundary, or a duplicated entity, both ROC and PR curves will look better than the model deserves, because the leakage inflates the underlying predictions before either metric even gets computed. Choosing the right curve is necessary but not sufficient; it has to sit on top of a leakage-aware split and a sensible train, validation, and test separation for the numbers to mean anything at all.

The honest habit I try to keep is to report both curves, state the class balance explicitly, and pick one or two concrete operating points that map onto an actual decision, such as how many false alarms an analyst can tolerate per day. That combination tends to survive scrutiny far better than a single headline AUC of either kind.
